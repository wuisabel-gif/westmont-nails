# Liquid Lacquer — an algorithmic philosophy

**Movement:** *Liquid Lacquer.* Polish is paint that wants to move. The instant it
leaves the brush it is already settling, levelling, catching light — a fluid caught
in the half-second before it becomes a surface. This system lives in that half-second.

**The computational worldview.** A field of invisible forces is laid across the canvas
with layered value noise — the same quiet mathematics that governs how a drop of lacquer
spreads across a nail bed. Hundreds of pigment particles are released into that field and
simply obey it, each one tracing the force lines as a thin, low-opacity stroke. No particle
knows the whole picture; the marbled swirl that emerges is the sum of their small, lawful
movements. Order from drift. The beauty is in the process, not any single frame.

**Pigment and gloss.** Colour is drawn only from the studio's own lacquers — deep wine,
dusty rose, champagne gold, blush, mauve — and laid down in translucent layers so the
strokes pool and overlap the way real polish does, building depth rather than opacity.
A continuous, almost imperceptible wash of the cream ground keeps the field from muddying,
so the composition stays soft and never crowds the words it sits behind. This is restraint
as craft: a meticulously tuned balance where the animation enriches the page without ever
competing with it.

**Seeded inevitability.** One seed fixes the whole field, so the swirl is reproducible yet
feels organic — each seed a different bottle from the same shelf. The reference is quiet by
design: anyone who has watched polish settle will feel it; everyone else simply reads it as
an elegant, living backdrop. The product of patient iteration, tuned stroke by stroke until
the motion felt like lacquer and nothing else.

**Stillness when asked.** For visitors who prefer reduced motion, the same algorithm runs
once and freezes — a single marbled still, painted by the identical forces, holding the
composition without movement. The system is built to breathe gently or hold its breath
entirely, and to do either beautifully.
